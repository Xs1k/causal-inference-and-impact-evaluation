Course Overview

This course provides a comprehensive introduction to modern methods in causal inference and applied econometrics. Students will learn the fundamental approaches to identifying causal effects in observational and experimental data, with emphasis on practical applications in economics and social sciences.

Prerequisites: Intermediate econometrics, statistics, and familiarity with regression analysis.

The course covers key identification strategies including randomized experiments, difference-in-differences, instrumental variables, regression discontinuity designs, and synthetic control methods. Each topic combines theoretical foundations with empirical applications from leading economic research.

Core Readings

  • Bloom, H.S. (2006). "The Core Analytics of Randomized Experiments for Social Research." MDRC Working Papers on Research Methodology
  • Stancheva, S. (2022). "How to Run Surveys: A guide to creating your identifying variation and revealing the invisible." Annual Review of Economics, 15(1): 205-234

Empirical Applications

  • Baird, S., McIntosh, C., and Özler, B. (2011). "Cash or Condition? Evidence from a Cash Transfer Experiment." The Quarterly Journal of Economics, 126(4): 1709-1753
    Randomized experiment in Malawi comparing conditional (CCT) and unconditional (UCT) cash transfers for adolescent girls. Finds CCT more effective for school enrollment but UCT better for reducing teen pregnancy and marriage, revealing important trade-offs in program design.
  • Fowlie, M., Greenstone, M., Wolfram, C. (2018). "Do Energy Efficiency Investments Deliver? Evidence from the Weatherization Assistance Program." The Quarterly Journal of Economics, 133(3): 1597-1644
    Experimental evaluation of ~30,000 households in the nation's largest residential energy efficiency program. Finds actual energy savings were only half of engineering model projections, and costs exceeded benefits with a negative return of -7.8% annually.
  • Alan, S., Corekcioglu, G., Sutter, M. (2023). "Improving Workplace Climate in Large Corporations: A Clustered Randomized Intervention." The Quarterly Journal of Economics, 138(1): 151-203
    Cluster-randomized experiment testing behavioral interventions to improve workplace climate in a large corporation, examining effects on cooperation and organizational culture.

4. Difference-in-Differences

Core Readings

  • MHE - Chapter 5
  • MT - Chapter 8
  • TE - Chapter 18

Empirical Applications

  • Qian, N. (2008). "Missing Women and the Price of Tea in China: The Effect of Sex-Specific Earnings on Sex Imbalance." Quarterly Journal of Economics, 123(3): 1251-1285
    Uses post-Mao agricultural reforms in China to study how sex-specific income affects child survival and education. Finds that increasing female income improves girls' survival rates and education, while increasing male income has the opposite effect, challenging unitary household models.
  • Duflo, E. (2001). "Schooling and Labor Market Consequences of School Construction in Indonesia: Evidence from an Unusual Policy Experiment." American Economic Review, 91(4): 795-813
    Exploits Indonesia's massive school construction program (1973-1979) to estimate returns to education. Finds each additional school per 1,000 children led to 0.12-0.19 more years of education and 1.5-5.8% higher wages.
  • Ebenstein, A., Lavy, V., and Roth, S. (2016). "The Long-Run Economic Consequences of High-Stakes Examinations: Evidence from Transitory Variation in Pollution." American Economic Journal: Applied Economics, 8(4): 36-65
    Uses daily variation in air pollution on high school matriculation exam dates in Israel to estimate long-run effects on university outcomes and earnings. Finds pollution-induced test score reductions have lasting impacts on educational attainment and labor market outcomes.
  • Arteaga, C. (2018). "The effect of human capital on earnings: Evidence from a reform at Colombia's top university." Journal of Public Economics, 157: 212-225
    Studies a reform at Universidad de los Andes that changed admission standards, using difference-in-differences to estimate returns to college quality and selectivity on earnings.

5. Instrumental Variables

Core Readings

  • MHE - Chapter 4
  • MT - Chapter 7
  • TE - Chapter 19

Empirical Applications

  • Angrist, J.D., and Evans, W.N. (1998). "Children and their parents' labor supply: Evidence from exogenous variation in family size." American Economic Review, 88(3): 450-477
    Uses sex composition of first two children as an instrument for family size to estimate effects on parental labor supply. Finds having a third child significantly reduces mothers' labor supply, with effects larger for less-educated women, but no effect on fathers' labor supply.
  • Guriev, S., Melnikov, N., and Zhuravskaya, E. (2021). "3G Internet and Confidence in Government." Quarterly Journal of Economics, 136(4): 2533-2613
    Exploits the staggered rollout of 3G mobile internet across Russia to study effects on political attitudes. Finds 3G access decreases confidence in government and trust in Putin, particularly in areas with alternative sources of information.
  • Aizer, A., and Doyle, J.J. (2015). "Juvenile Incarceration, Human Capital, and Future Crime: Evidence from Randomly Assigned Judges." The Quarterly Journal of Economics, 130(2): 759-803
    Uses judge assignment as an instrument for juvenile incarceration to estimate its causal effects. Finds incarceration decreases high school completion and increases adult incarceration, challenging the assumption that detention reduces future crime.

6. Regression Discontinuity

Core Readings

  • MHE - Chapter 6
  • MT - Chapter 6
  • TE - Chapter 20
  • Cattaneo, M., Idrobo, N., and Titiunik, R. (2019). "A Practical Introduction to Regression Discontinuity Designs: Foundations."
  • Cattaneo, M., Idrobo, N., and Titiunik, R. (2023). "A Practical Introduction to Regression Discontinuity Designs: Extensions."
  • Lee, D.S., and Lemieux, T. (2010). "Regression Discontinuity Designs in Economics." Journal of Economic Literature, 48(2): 281-355

Empirical Applications

  • Urquiola, M. (2006). "Identifying Class Size Effects in Developing Countries: Evidence from Rural Bolivia." Review of Economics and Statistics, 88(1): 171-177 (Note: the working paper version of 2003 is better)
    Uses class size cutoff rules in rural Bolivian schools as a regression discontinuity design to estimate effects of class size on student achievement in developing countries.
  • Pinotti, P. (2017). "Clicking on Heaven's Door: The effect of Immigrant Legalization on Crime." American Economic Review, 107(1): 138-168
    Exploits Italy's electronic residence permit application system as an RDD. Fixed quotas processed on first-come, first-served basis create sharp cutoff. Finds legalization reduces immigrant crime rate by 0.6 percentage points (from 1.1% baseline), demonstrating importance of legal status.
  • Fort, M., Ichino, A., and Zanella, G. (2020). "Cognitive and Noncognitive Costs of Day Care at Age 0-2 for Children in Advantaged Families." Journal of Political Economy, 128(1): 158-205
    Uses lottery-based assignment to day care facilities in Bologna as RDD to study effects of early childcare on child development outcomes in advantaged families.
  • Greenstone, M., and Gallagher, J. (2008). "Does Hazardous Waste Matter? Evidence from the Housing Market and the Superfund Program." Quarterly Journal of Economics, 123(3): 951-1003
    Uses discontinuity in EPA's Superfund site selection based on hazard ranking scores to estimate housing market impacts of environmental contamination and cleanup.
